I am Hugo Aerts, a professor, scientist, and builder working to create a future where artificial intelligence helps us better understand, treat, and prevent disease.

From AI Breakthroughs to Patient Impact

Artificial intelligence is one of the most important technological breakthroughs of our time. Yet for all of its promise, its impact on everyday medicine has so far fallen short of expectations.

I believe the reason is simple: healthcare is not fundamentally a technology problem — it is a biology problem. Real progress will come not from building larger models alone, but from using AI to uncover new biological insights, rigorously validating them, and translating them into clinical practice.

My mission is to bridge that gap. By combining artificial intelligence, medicine, and scientific discovery, I work to uncover new insights into cancer, aging, and human health that can ultimately improve the prevention, diagnosis, and treatment of disease.

Research
Cancer
Cancer remains one of the greatest challenges in medicine. My research focuses on developing new approaches to understand cancer biology, predict treatment response, and personalize care. By applying artificial intelligence to clinical data, we have shown that routine data contain a wealth of biological information that was previously invisible to physicians. These discoveries have helped establish new opportunities for precision oncology.
Aging
People age at different rates, yet medicine largely relies on chronological age rather than biological age. My work seeks to better understand the biological processes that drive aging and resilience. Through advances in artificial intelligence, we have demonstrated that routine medical images contain measurable signals of biological aging that are associated with health outcomes, disease risk, and survival. Our goal is to develop new tools that help identify risk earlier and promote healthier aging.
Immune Health
The immune system plays a central role in cancer, aging, infection, and chronic disease, yet many aspects of immune health remain poorly understood and difficult to measure. Our recent work has challenged long-standing assumptions about the adult thymus, revealing that this often-overlooked organ remains strongly associated with mortality, cancer risk, cardiovascular disease, and response to immunotherapy. By developing new ways to measure immune health, we aim to better understand resilience, disease susceptibility, and treatment outcomes across the lifespan.
AI Foundation Models
Artificial intelligence is transforming how we analyze and interpret medical data. My research has helped pioneer the use of AI and machine learning in medical data, from deep learning to foundation models. While recent advances in AI have demonstrated extraordinary potential, meaningful clinical impact requires rigorous validation, biological understanding, and real-world implementation. My goal is to bridge the gap between technological breakthroughs and patient care, ensuring that advances in AI translate into measurable improvements in human health.

About

My name is Hugo Aerts (pronounced 'Arts'), and I am a professor, scientist, and builder dedicated to improving human health through scientific discovery and artificial intelligence.

Over the past two decades, my work has focused on uncovering biological signals hidden within medical data. This research has contributed to the development of foundation models for medicine, and led to new insights into cancer, aging, and immune health. More recently, my team has challenged long-standing assumptions about the adult thymus, revealing its important role in cancer, aging, and overall health.

I currently serve as Professor at Harvard University and Director of the AI in Medicine Program at Mass General Brigham, where I lead multidisciplinary teams of scientists, clinicians, and engineers working at the intersection of artificial intelligence, medicine, and biology.

I believe we are at a unique moment in history. Artificial intelligence has demonstrated extraordinary potential, yet meaningful transformation of healthcare remains limited. The challenge is no longer simply building better algorithms, but using these technologies to generate new biological insights, rigorously validate discoveries, and translate them into clinical practice. My work focuses on bridging that gap.

My long-term goal is to help create a future in which medicine can better understand, predict, and prevent disease by measuring the biological processes that shape health, aging, resilience, and treatment response. By combining advances in AI, imaging, and biomedical science, I hope to contribute to a deeper understanding of human biology and ultimately improve outcomes for patients worldwide.

Originally from the Netherlands and now based in Boston, I continue to be inspired by the convergence of science, technology, and medicine.
